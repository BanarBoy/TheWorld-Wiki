# Driftwood

**Type:** Port Settlement / Smuggler Haven  
**Continent:** [[Valecaryn]]  
**Region:** Northwestern Frontier (Western Coast)  
**River / Coast:** Open western coastline with shoals, wreck-strewn shallows, and shifting tides  
**Population:** ~2,000–3,500 (highly transient)  
**Elevation:** Low coastal flats with slight rises inland  
**Government:** Informal leadership / competing factions  
**Founded:** ~70–100 years ago from wreck survivors and opportunists  
**Strategic Role:** Unofficial trade hub between frontier settlements along the western coast

---

# Overview

Driftwood is less a city than a consequence.

Built along a dangerous stretch of the western coast of [[Valecaryn]]’s northwestern frontier, it occupies a shoreline where currents shift unpredictably and submerged hazards claim ships with quiet regularity. What began as a refuge for survivors and scavengers grew into something more enduring—though never fully stable. Each wreck added to it. Each storm reshaped it.

The settlement has no unified authority and no singular purpose beyond opportunity. It serves as a gathering point for those operating outside formal systems—smugglers, independent traders, wreck divers, and those looking to disappear between more structured ports like Saltpine and Alder’s Retreat. Goods flow through Driftwood that would not pass cleanly through regulated harbors.

To outsiders, Driftwood is known as unreliable but useful. It is a place where lost cargo resurfaces, where unofficial routes begin or end, and where information travels as quickly as goods. Trust is conditional, and reputation is everything.

Despite the instability of both its environment and its people, Driftwood persists—not through strength or order, but through adaptation. It exists because the coast demands it.

---

# Physical Geography

## Regional Context

Driftwood lies along the western coastline of the northwestern frontier of [[Valecaryn]], positioned between more stable settlements but directly along one of the least predictable maritime stretches in the region.

Unlike the relatively sheltered inlet of Alder’s Retreat or the functional harbor of Saltpine, Driftwood occupies exposed coastline shaped by irregular currents and submerged debris. This makes it a natural hazard zone for passing ships—and a natural opportunity for those willing to exploit it.

Its location places it in a gray space between influence and neglect. It is close enough to established trade routes to remain relevant, but far enough from centralized authority to avoid consistent oversight.

---

## Immediate Terrain

Driftwood is built on unstable coastal ground—sand, gravel, and compacted debris layered over time. Much of the settlement rests on reinforced platforms constructed from salvaged timber, ship remnants, and whatever materials can be repurposed.

The shoreline is jagged and uneven, broken by exposed rock, tidal flats, and the remains of old wrecks embedded in the shallows. At low tide, large sections of debris become visible, forming a shifting extension of the settlement itself.

Inland, the ground rises slightly, offering marginally more stability. These areas host more permanent structures, though even they are subject to erosion and shifting soil over time.

Nothing in Driftwood feels entirely fixed.

---

## Hydrology

Driftwood operates without a reliable natural freshwater source.

Water is obtained through:

• Shallow, often brackish wells  
• Rain collection systems  
• Imported supply from nearby settlements

The surrounding sea is heavily tidal, with dramatic shifts exposing hazards and pathways throughout the day. Locals rely on this knowledge to navigate, salvage, and sometimes ambush.

Flooding is common in lower areas during storms, with sections of the settlement periodically submerged or damaged.

---

## Climate

Driftwood is fully exposed to the western coastal climate of the northwestern frontier.

**Spring**  
Cold, wet, and fog-heavy. Visibility is often poor, making navigation hazardous.

**Summer**  
Mild but unpredictable. Calm conditions can shift rapidly into wind and rain. Maritime activity increases despite the risks.

**Autumn**  
Storm season. High winds and violent seas drive shipwrecks along the coast, making this the most active period for salvage and opportunistic trade.

**Winter**  
Severe and isolating. Freezing winds and relentless storms reduce traffic, though experienced crews continue to operate when conditions allow.

# Ecology

## Flora

### Native Vegetation

Vegetation around Driftwood is sparse and resilient, shaped by salt exposure, poor soil, and constant wind.

Coastal grasses dominate the immediate shoreline, clinging to dunes and low ground with tangled root systems that help stabilize the shifting terrain. These grasses grow in uneven patches, bending almost permanently in the direction of prevailing winds.

Farther inland, scrub brush and low, twisted shrubs appear—stunted by harsh conditions but persistent. Occasional wind-warped pines and driftwood-rooted growths mark the transition toward more stable ground, though true forest lies some distance away.

Plant life here does not grow tall—it endures.

---

### Agricultural Landscape

Agriculture is nearly nonexistent within Driftwood itself.

The soil is too poor, too unstable, and too salt-saturated to support meaningful cultivation. Small personal gardens exist in sheltered inland pockets, but these are limited and unreliable.

Most food is obtained through fishing, trade, or salvage rather than farming.

---

### Economically Important Plants

**Coastal Grasses** — Used for weaving rope, insulation, and basic matting.

**Driftwood Growths** — Small, hardy plants that take root in accumulated debris; occasionally used for medicinal or practical purposes.

**Imported Crops** — Any significant plant-based goods are brought in from elsewhere and hold higher value as a result.

---

## Fauna

### Common Wildlife

Seabirds dominate the area—gulls, scavenger birds, and larger coastal predators that circle constantly over the shoreline and docks.

Crabs, shellfish, and other tidepool creatures are abundant, forming a reliable food source. Fish are plentiful, though conditions make consistent harvesting difficult.

Rats and other scavengers are common within the settlement itself, feeding on refuse and cargo remnants.

---

### Predators and Dangerous Creatures

The sea is the primary source of danger.

Large, unseen shapes beneath the water are occasionally reported, particularly during storms or at low visibility. Whether these are natural predators or something less understood is unclear.

On land, danger is less about wildlife and more about environment—unstable footing, collapsing structures, and shifting debris fields.

---

### Domesticated Animals

Domesticated animals are limited due to lack of space and resources.

• A small number of hardy dogs (used for guarding or companionship)  
• Occasional goats in more stable inland areas  
• Chickens kept in enclosed spaces where possible

Animals are kept only when they provide clear utility.

---

# Urban Structure

## City Layout

Driftwood has no true layout—only layers.

The settlement is built outward and upward from the shoreline, with structures added wherever space allows. Walkways twist between buildings, some elevated on stilts or platforms to avoid flooding, others resting directly on unstable ground.

Paths are inconsistent, often shifting as structures are added, removed, or collapse. What appears to be a main route one season may be blocked or rerouted the next.

The harbor is not a defined space, but a collection of usable docking points, tied together by habit rather than design.

---

## Districts

Driftwood does not formally recognize districts, but certain areas are functionally distinct:

### The Breakwater

A loose cluster of docks and anchored vessels forming the primary point of entry. Ships here are often repaired, stripped, or repurposed rather than simply docked.

### The Wreckline

The outer edge of the settlement where debris fields meet the sea. Salvage operations are constant, and structures here are the least stable.

### The Stack

A dense cluster of multi-level structures built atop one another inland. This is where most permanent residents live, though “permanent” is a relative term.

### The Dry Rise

Slightly elevated ground inland, hosting the most stable buildings—storehouses, meeting spaces, and the few structures that resemble long-term construction.

---

## Architecture

Architecture in Driftwood is entirely improvised.

Buildings are constructed from salvaged materials—ship planks, broken masts, stone fragments, and whatever else can be recovered. No two structures are the same, though many share a similar patchwork appearance.

Reinforcement is inconsistent. Some buildings are surprisingly sturdy, while others feel temporary at best.

Roofs are uneven, often layered or repaired multiple times. Walkways between structures are common, forming a vertical as well as horizontal network.

Nothing is built to last.

Everything is built to work—until it doesn’t.

---

# Infrastructure

## Roads and Transportation

There are no formal roads within Driftwood.

Movement is handled through:

• Narrow paths between structures  
• Elevated walkways and planks  
• Direct shoreline travel during low tide

Navigation relies on familiarity. Outsiders often find the settlement confusing or disorienting.

Maritime transport is the primary means of connection to the outside world.

---

## Water Systems

Water is scarce and carefully managed.

• Rain collection systems are widespread  
• Wells exist but are often unreliable or brackish  
• Clean water is a traded commodity

Storage containers are a common feature of nearly every structure.

---

## Food Supply

Driftwood survives through a combination of:

• Fishing and tidepool harvesting  
• Trade with nearby settlements  
• Salvaged goods from wrecks

Food availability fluctuates. Periods of abundance can be followed by scarcity depending on weather and trade conditions.

---

## Defenses

Driftwood has no formal defenses—and does not need them in a traditional sense.

Its protection comes from:

• Difficult approach through hazardous waters  
• Lack of centralized targets  
• A population accustomed to conflict

Any organized attack would struggle to navigate both the terrain and the people defending it.

Defense here is chaotic, decentralized, and effective in its own way.

---
# Economy

## Major Industries

Driftwood’s economy is built on three things: salvage, smuggling, and opportunistic trade.

Salvage is the foundation. Shipwrecks along the coast provide a steady, if unpredictable, flow of goods—timber, metal, cargo, and sometimes entire hull sections that can be repurposed. Crews specialize in recovering materials quickly before storms or tides reclaim them.

Smuggling operates alongside this, using Driftwood as a transfer point for goods that cannot—or should not—move through formal ports. Items are rarely stored long; they pass through quickly, changing hands multiple times before leaving the settlement.

Fishing and small-scale trade supplement these activities, but they are secondary. The true economy lies in what others have lost—or wish to move unseen.

---

## Trade

Driftwood trades with anyone willing to accept its terms.

Exports include:

• Salvaged cargo (often untraceable)  
• Reclaimed ship materials  
• Illicit or untaxed goods  
• Information

Imports include:

• Food and fresh water  
• Tools and repair materials  
• Alcohol and consumables  
• Anything with resale value

Trade routes are informal and constantly shifting. Deals are based on trust, leverage, and reputation rather than contracts.

---

## Markets and Commerce

There is no central market—only points of exchange.

Transactions occur:

• Dockside, as ships arrive or depart  
• Within temporary stalls or makeshift structures  
• In taverns, where negotiations often begin or end

Prices fluctuate constantly based on availability, urgency, and who is asking.

Currency is used, but barter is common—especially when dealing with salvaged goods.

---

## Labor and Guilds

There are no guilds, only crews and affiliations.

Labor is organized through:

• Salvage teams  
• Smuggling networks  
• Independent operators

Loyalty is fluid. Individuals move between groups based on opportunity, though some crews develop strong internal bonds.

Skill is recognized quickly, and reputation spreads fast. A capable individual can rise rapidly—but can fall just as quickly.

---

# Society and Culture

## Cultural Identity

Driftwood is defined by adaptability and self-interest.

There is no shared ideology, no unifying belief system, and no expectation of cohesion beyond what is necessary to function. People come to Driftwood to operate outside constraints—legal, social, or otherwise.

Despite this, a form of order exists.

It is not imposed—it emerges. Certain lines are understood without being spoken. Crossing them carries consequences, not from law, but from the collective response of those affected.

Trust is conditional. Respect is earned. Weakness is noticed.

---

## Social Hierarchy

Hierarchy exists, but it is unstable.

• Influential crew leaders and established operators  
• Skilled specialists (salvagers, navigators, negotiators)  
• General labor and transient workers  
• Outsiders and newcomers

Power shifts frequently, tied to recent success rather than long-term position.

Reputation is the closest thing Driftwood has to status.

---

## Daily Life

Life in Driftwood is irregular.

There is no consistent schedule. Activity rises and falls with the tides, the weather, and the arrival of ships. A quiet morning can become chaotic within moments if a wreck is spotted or a valuable shipment arrives.

Work is intense and immediate when it comes, followed by periods of waiting, negotiation, or rest.

Evenings are rarely quiet. Taverns and gathering spaces remain active late into the night, serving as centers of both social life and business.

---

## Festivals and Holidays

There are no formal celebrations.

Moments of success—large hauls, profitable deals, survival after storms—become their own form of celebration. These are spontaneous, loud, and short-lived.

There is no shared calendar—only shared opportunity.

---

## Cuisine

Food is inconsistent but often plentiful in bursts.

Common meals include:

• Fresh or preserved fish  
• Shellfish and tidepool harvests  
• Imported grains and bread  
• Whatever can be salvaged or traded

Quality varies widely. A good day can bring unexpected luxury; a bad stretch reduces meals to the bare minimum.

---

## Clothing

Clothing in Driftwood is practical and varied.

• Heavy coats and layered fabrics for coastal weather  
• Waterproofed materials where available  
• Mixed styles reflecting different origins

There is no uniformity. Clothing is often patched, repurposed, or altered over time.

---

# Religion

Religion in Driftwood is fragmented and largely personal.

There are no major temples or organized institutions. Individuals may carry symbols, perform rituals, or follow beliefs brought from elsewhere, but these are rarely shared or enforced.

Small shrines or tokens can be found in isolated corners—dedications to the sea, luck, or survival—but they are private rather than communal.

Belief exists, but it is not a unifying force.

---

# Government and Politics

## Political Structure

Driftwood has no formal government.

Instead, it operates through a shifting balance of influence among powerful individuals and groups. Decisions affecting the settlement are typically made through negotiation, pressure, or direct action rather than structured process.

---

## Power Centers

• Established salvage crews  
• Smuggling networks  
• Individuals with control over key resources (water, docking access, information)

Power is decentralized and constantly contested.

---

## Law and Order

There is no formal legal system, but there are consequences.

Disputes are resolved through:

• Direct negotiation  
• Reputation-based enforcement  
• Violence, when necessary

Certain behaviors—such as interfering with active salvage or betraying established agreements—are widely understood to be unacceptable, even without written rules.

Order exists, but it is enforced socially rather than institutionally.

---

# Military

## Garrison

There is no standing force.

However, many individuals in Driftwood are experienced fighters, either from maritime work or past conflicts. In times of threat, resistance forms quickly and unpredictably.

---

## Strategic Importance

Driftwood’s importance lies in its position outside formal systems.

It provides:

• A hub for unofficial trade  
• A fallback point for ships avoiding regulated ports  
• Access to goods and information that cannot be obtained elsewhere

Controlling Driftwood would be difficult—and holding it would be even harder.

Its strength is not in defense, but in its refusal to be structured.

# Demographics

Driftwood’s population is fluid, inconsistent, and difficult to measure with precision.

Humans make up the majority, drawn from across [[Valecaryn]] and beyond—sailors, smugglers, laborers, and those with reasons not to remain in more structured settlements. Unlike places such as Alder’s Retreat, Driftwood does not filter who stays. Presence alone is enough.

Non-human populations are more visible here than in neighboring frontier settlements. Individuals of mixed or distant lineage, including those with faint elven or other heritage, pass through or remain without much notice. Driftwood does not question origin unless it becomes relevant.

Population shifts constantly. Ships arrive, crews disband, individuals vanish, and others take their place. Some residents have remained for years. Others stay only long enough to disappear again.

No one is tracked.

---

# History

## Founding

Driftwood was not founded in any formal sense.

It began as a temporary refuge along a hazardous stretch of coast where shipwreck survivors gathered, repaired what they could, and salvaged what remained. The location proved consistently valuable—not because it was safe, but because it wasn’t.

Where ships failed, resources accumulated.

Over time, what began as scattered camps became a semi-permanent presence. Structures were built from wreckage, then reinforced, then expanded. Each storm added to it.

---

## Growth

Growth in Driftwood has never been planned.

It expands in response to opportunity—new wrecks, new trade routes, new people. Sections of the settlement appear, disappear, or shift depending on conditions and need.

There have been periods of rapid expansion following particularly active storm seasons, as well as periods of decline when resources became scarce or conflict drove people away.

Despite this instability, Driftwood has never fully collapsed.

It adapts.

---

## Wars and Conflicts

Driftwood has not been the site of formal warfare, but conflict is constant at smaller scales.

• Clashes between competing crews  
• Disputes over salvage rights  
• Retaliation against betrayal or theft  
• Occasional attempts by outside forces to impose control

These conflicts are rarely organized or prolonged. They are immediate, decisive, and quickly absorbed back into the rhythm of the settlement.

Attempts to control Driftwood from the outside have consistently failed. The environment and the population resist sustained authority.

---

## Recent Developments

Recently, activity along the coast has increased.

More wrecks have been reported—some under conditions that do not align with normal weather patterns. Ships arriving in Driftwood carry stories of sudden disorientation, shifting currents, or storms that appear without warning.

At the same time, certain crews within Driftwood have begun to consolidate influence, controlling access to key salvage zones and docking areas. While this has not yet formed a unified authority, it represents a shift toward something more structured—whether intentional or not.

There are also rumors that some wrecks are not accidents.

• Independent brokers who facilitate high-risk trade  
• Long-standing residents who understand the settlement’s shifting dynamics

Driftwood produces names that matter locally—but rarely beyond it. Reputation here does not travel cleanly into the outside world.

---

# Notable Organizations

• Salvage crews and alliances  
• Smuggling networks  
• Informal trade circles

These groups form, dissolve, and reform constantly. Stability is temporary.

---

# Reputation

Driftwood is known across the western coast as a place where anything can be found—if one is willing to accept the cost.

Among sailors, it is:

• A last resort for repairs or supplies  
• A place to recover lost goods—or lose more  
• A harbor that exists, but cannot be relied upon

Among merchants, it is:

• A useful but dangerous intermediary  
• A source of unregulated goods  
• A place best dealt with indirectly

Among those who live there, it is simply:

Opportunity.

# Sights and Landmarks

• The Wreckline — A shifting field of broken hulls and debris exposed at low tide  
• The Breakwater — A chaotic cluster of docks and anchored vessels  
• The Stack — Layered structures rising unevenly inland  
• The Dry Rise — The most stable ground, hosting semi-permanent buildings  
• Embedded Hulls — Old ships partially buried in sand, now part of the settlement itself

---

# Sounds, Smells, and Atmosphere

Driftwood is never still.

The constant creak of wood—ships, structures, walkways—blends with the crash of waves and the calls of seabirds. Voices rise and fall unpredictably, negotiations turning to arguments and back again without warning.

The air smells of salt, rot, tar, and wet timber. At low tide, the scent of exposed debris and marine life intensifies, thick and unavoidable.

The atmosphere is unstable but familiar to those who understand it. Nothing is guaranteed, but everything follows a kind of logic—one that rewards awareness and punishes complacency.

To outsiders, it feels chaotic.

To those who belong, it feels precise.

# Rumors and Conflicts

• Some wrecks arrive with cargo untouched—but crews missing entirely  
• Certain individuals seem to know where wrecks will occur before they happen  
• A growing faction is attempting to control access to key salvage zones  
• The waters offshore behave inconsistently, even in calm weather  
• There are whispers that something beneath the surface is influencing the currents

A crew claims to know where the next wreck will occur—and offers to sell the information.

**The Empty Ship**  
A vessel drifts into the Wreckline intact, cargo untouched, but with no crew aboard and no signs of struggle.

**The Rising Power**  
A coalition of crews begins enforcing control over parts of Driftwood, threatening the settlement’s balance.

**Currents That Don’t Align**  
Navigational charts begin to fail as currents shift unpredictably, leading to questions about what is changing—and why.
