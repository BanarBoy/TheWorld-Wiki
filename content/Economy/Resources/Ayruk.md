# Overview

Ayruk is a fermented drink made from mare’s milk, serving as a staple of daily life among the steppe peoples of northern Kiyora. Lightly alcoholic and slightly sour, it provides hydration, nutrition, and a means of preserving milk in an environment where long-term storage of fresh food is difficult.

Produced through natural fermentation, Ayruk transforms perishable milk into a more stable and transportable form. It can be carried over long distances, consumed throughout the day, and shared among riders and families alike. For many, it is not simply a beverage, but a constant presence—something drunk as naturally as water.

Its importance extends beyond practicality. Ayruk is deeply tied to identity, hospitality, and social interaction. It is offered to guests, shared during gatherings, and used in rites and celebrations. Refusing Ayruk, in many contexts, is seen as a rejection of goodwill.

Regional variation gives Ayruk additional character. Ayruk produced in [[Sujun]] is often richer due to dense herds, while batches fermented along the colder routes of [[Khordai]] are sharper and more potent. Among riders traveling through [[Uluur]], Ayruk is often maintained continuously in motion, producing a distinct, well-mixed consistency prized by experienced clans.

# Geographic Distribution

Ayruk is produced across a connected steppe system:

• the grazing plains of [[Tsetsui]]  
• the dense herd basins of [[Sujun]]  
• the central cultural zones near [[Harakum]]  
• the highland regions of [[Khordai]]  
• migration corridors through [[Uluur]]  
• seasonal gathering zones near [[Tarkhul]]

It depends on:

• milk from **Ulaari Steeds**, particularly large herds in [[Uluur]] and [[Tsetsui]]  
• access to fermentation vessels carried by nomadic groups  
• continuous movement and daily production cycles

These regions support:

• large horse populations  
• uninterrupted milk supply  
• integration of fermentation into travel

Because of its nature:

• Ayruk is produced wherever herds move  
• its character changes depending on region and season

# Production

Ayruk is produced through a simple but carefully maintained process integrated into nomadic life.

---

### Milk Collection

• fresh mare’s milk is collected daily from herds, especially in [[Uluur]] and [[Tsetsui]]  
• quality varies based on grazing conditions and herd health

---

### Fermentation

• milk is stored in portable containers  
• agitation from travel—especially across [[Uluur]]—naturally mixes and ferments the liquid  
• cultures develop over time, often unique to specific clans

---

### Maintenance

• batches are continuously refreshed  
• Ayruk from [[Harakum]] is often more balanced due to stable encampments  
• Ayruk from [[Khordai]] may ferment longer, producing stronger variants

Production is:

• ongoing and cyclical  
• shaped by movement and environment  
• defined more by tradition than strict method

# Processing and Transport

---

### Processing

Ayruk exists in several regionally influenced forms:

• **Fresh Ayruk** — mild, widely consumed in [[Harakum]] and [[Sujun]]  
• **Strong Ayruk** — more fermented, common in colder regions like [[Khordai]]  
• **Travel Ayruk** — continuously mixed during movement through [[Uluur]]  
• **Dried Ayruk Curds** — preserved form often prepared near [[Tarkhul]] for long campaigns

---

### Transport

Ayruk is transported:

• in leather or felt-lined containers made from **Kharu Felt**  
• carried by riders during travel  
• maintained as a living mixture rather than a static product

Because of its nature:

• fresh Ayruk remains local  
• preserved forms from [[Tarkhul]] travel further

# Economic Importance

Ayruk is a **daily subsistence resource** that supports mobility.

It:

• provides essential nutrition across all regions  
• sustains riders during long-distance travel  
• reduces reliance on stored food or water

Regional roles include:

• [[Sujun]] — high-volume production from dense herds  
• [[Tsetsui]] — widespread herd-based supply  
• [[Harakum]] — cultural center of consumption and exchange  
• [[Tarkhul]] — preparation of preserved forms for campaigns

It is:

> not a luxury, but a constant necessity

---

# Controlling Powers

Control is decentralized but influenced by region.

Primary actors include:

• herding families in [[Sujun]] and [[Tsetsui]]  
• clans maintaining fermentation traditions in [[Harakum]]  
• traveling groups sustaining production through [[Uluur]]

Power is determined by:

• herd access  
• consistency of production  
• cultural reputation

---

# Trade Networks

Ayruk moves through:

• internal steppe networks between clans  
• seasonal exchanges near [[Tarkhul]]  
• limited trade into settled regions in preserved form

Trade characteristics:

• highly local  
• difficult to export fresh  
• more viable as dried or concentrated product

# Cultural Significance

Ayruk represents:

• hospitality  
• continuity  
• shared life across regions

It is:

• offered to guests in every territory  
• consumed across all environments—from [[Sujun]] to [[Khordai]]  
• a constant presence regardless of movement

To the steppe peoples:

> to share Ayruk is to share trust

# Dangers and Limitations

• dependent on healthy horse herds  
• spoilage risk in warmer regions like [[Sujun]] if mishandled  
• over-fermentation in colder zones like [[Khordai]]

Additionally:

• contamination can spread quickly through shared batches  
• disruption of herds in [[Uluur]] affects widespread supply
