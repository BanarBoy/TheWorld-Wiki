# Overview

Lianmi is the foundational staple crop of Kiyora, forming the dietary and economic backbone of the region. Cultivated across carefully managed floodplains and terraced fields, it supports dense population centers and enables long-term agricultural stability.

Among foreign traders and neighboring regions, Lianmi is most commonly known as **“Kiyoran Rice,”** a term used to distinguish it from other grains and to emphasize its association with Kiyora’s highly controlled agricultural systems. While the name simplifies its identity, it reflects the crop’s reputation for consistency, quality, and reliability.

Unlike hardier grains grown in harsher climates, Lianmi depends on precision. Its cultivation requires controlled water flow, consistent seasonal cycles, and coordinated labor. When properly managed, it produces reliable, high-yield harvests capable of sustaining both rural communities and major urban populations.

Its importance extends beyond sustenance. Lianmi defines land use, labor organization, and governance throughout Kiyora. Entire administrative systems exist to regulate irrigation, allocate water rights, and maintain field networks. In this way, Lianmi is not merely a crop—it is the organizing principle of Kiyoran society.

While widely consumed domestically, surplus production is processed into durable forms for trade, allowing Kiyora to export not just food, but stability.

# Geographic Distribution

Lianmi is cultivated in:

• the floodplains surrounding [[Qinghe]] within the **Qinghe basin**  
• the inland basin and water systems near [[Minghu]] within the **Minghe basin**  
• terraced hillsides and managed slopes around [[Tianzhou]] within the **Fenghe basin**  
• interconnected irrigation corridors linking these river systems

It thrives in:

• shallow, controlled standing water  
• nutrient-rich alluvial soil  
• warm growing seasons with stable rainfall

These regions are ideal because:

• river systems provide consistent irrigation  
• terrain supports terrace construction  
• water flow can be precisely regulated and redistributed

Lianmi cultivation is inseparable from:

• engineered irrigation networks  
• seasonal water management  
• coordinated regional labor systems

# Production

Lianmi production is highly structured and labor-intensive.

---

### Field Preparation

• fields are leveled and flooded through irrigation channels  
• soil is prepared to retain water evenly  
• terrace systems are maintained to prevent runoff and erosion

---

### Planting

• seedlings are grown in controlled nursery plots  
• transplanted into flooded paddies by hand  
• planting occurs in synchronized cycles across regions

---

### Cultivation

• water depth is carefully regulated  
• fields are monitored for pests and disease  
• labor is coordinated across large farming collectives

---

### Harvesting

• Lianmi is harvested manually or with simple tools  
• cut, bundled, and dried in controlled conditions  
• grains are separated and stored immediately

Production is:

• high-yield when stable  
• dependent on infrastructure  
• vulnerable to disruption in water systems

# Processing and Transport

---

### Processing

Lianmi is processed into several forms:

• husked grain for daily consumption  
• polished Lianmi for higher-status markets  
• Lianmi flour for cooking and trade  
• fermented products used in preservation and cuisine

---

### Transport

Lianmi is distributed through:

• river barges across the **Qinghe**, **Minghe**, and **Fenghe** systems  
• canal networks linking [[Qinghe]] and [[Minghu]]  
• overland routes to urban centers such as [[Laoshan]] and [[Tianzhou]]

For export:

• processed and dried Lianmi is transported in bulk  
• sealed to prevent moisture damage  
• distributed through controlled trade networks

---

# Economic Importance

Lianmi is the **primary foundation of Kiyora’s economy**.

It:

• sustains the majority of the population  
• supports urban centers such as [[Laoshan]]  
• stabilizes labor and production systems  
• enables surplus-based trade

Key dependencies include:

• [[Qinghe]] — major agricultural production center (Qinghe basin)  
• [[Minghu]] — processing and redistribution hub (Minghe basin)  
• [[Tianzhou]] — terrace-based cultivation (Fenghe basin)  
• [[Laoshan]] — administrative and consumption center (Laohe basin)

It is:

> both common and indispensable

A failure of Lianmi production would:

• destabilize food supply  
• disrupt governance systems  
• threaten regional stability

---

# Controlling Powers

Control of Lianmi is tied directly to water and infrastructure.

Primary actors include:

• **river authorities** — regulate water flow and irrigation access  
• agricultural ministries — coordinate planting, quotas, and taxation  
• farming collectives — manage labor and local production  
• regional officials — enforce distribution and storage policies

Power is determined by:

• access to irrigation systems  
• control of water allocation  
• ability to maintain agricultural infrastructure

---

# Trade Networks

Lianmi is distributed:

• across all regions of [[Kiyora]] via interconnected river systems  
• to urban centers through canal and overland networks  
• in limited quantities to foreign markets, where it is known as **Kiyoran Rice**

Exports are:

• secondary to domestic need  
• focused on preserved or refined forms

Trade routes include:

• river transport across the Qinghe–Minghe–Fenghe network  
• inland redistribution through [[Minghu]]  
• controlled export through coastal trade hubs

# Dangers and Limitations

• dependence on stable irrigation systems  
• vulnerability to flooding or drought  
• labor-intensive cultivation  
• risk of pests and crop disease

Additionally:

• disruption of water networks can collapse production  
• political mismanagement of irrigation can lead to conflict
