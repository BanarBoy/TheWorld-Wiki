# Overview

Masari Resin is a thick, aromatic substance harvested from select trees within the jungles of the [[Masari Archipelago]]. Drawn from carefully cut channels in the bark, the resin seeps slowly before hardening into golden or amber-like masses that retain a powerful, lingering scent when burned or processed.

Its fragrance is complex—warm, slightly sweet, with smoky and mineral undertones shaped by the volcanic soils of the islands. This makes it highly desirable for both religious and commercial uses, particularly as incense and as a fixative in perfumes, where it stabilizes and extends more delicate scents.

Though harvested locally, Masari Resin is deeply tied to Seluqar’s economic sphere. While the islands are not formally annexed, Seluqar merchant houses exert dominant influence over trade, pricing, and export routes. As a result, Resin is both a symbol of Masari identity and a quiet engine of Seluqar wealth.

---

# Geographic Distribution

Masari Resin is produced exclusively in:

- dense jungle regions of the [[Masari Archipelago]]
- interior groves with mature resin-bearing trees
- volcanic soil zones that enhance aromatic properties

It requires:

- high humidity
- stable tree growth over many years
- specific tree species adapted to Masari conditions

Different islands produce slightly different resin profiles, contributing to variations in scent and value.

---

# Production

Resin harvesting is a slow and skilled process.

Production involves:

- scoring tree bark in controlled patterns
- allowing resin to seep and harden over time
- collecting hardened resin without damaging the tree

Key production zones:

- clan-controlled groves throughout the Masari islands
- organized harvesting networks supplying [[Marakai]]

Harvesting is:

- cyclical rather than continuous
- dependent on tree health
- managed to avoid long-term damage

Overharvesting can permanently reduce yield or kill trees.

---

# Processing and Transport

Masari Resin is minimally processed at the source.

Processing includes:

- cleaning and sorting by color and purity
- breaking into transportable chunks or refining into paste
- distillation into oils for high-end perfume use

Transport methods:

- moved from jungle groves to coastal hubs
- consolidated in [[Marakai]]
- exported via Seluqar-controlled maritime routes

Because of its density and value, Resin is easy to transport relative to bulk goods.

---

# Economic Importance

Masari Resin is a **cultural and commercial luxury resource**.

It is essential for:

- incense use in temples and port cities
- perfume production in Seluqar and beyond
- ritual practices across Thalassar

Key dependencies:

- Seluqar perfume and trade industries rely on it
- temples and religious institutions consume it regularly
- Masari local economies depend on its harvest

While not essential for survival, it is deeply embedded in both daily life and elite culture.

---

# Controlling Powers

Control of Masari Resin is layered and politically sensitive:

- Masari clans control harvesting knowledge and grove access
- local networks manage initial collection
- Seluqar merchant houses dominate export and pricing
- Marakai port authorities regulate flow of goods

This creates a **de facto Seluqar economic dominance** without formal political control.

---

# Trade Networks

Masari Resin flows through:

- jungle harvesting routes to [[Marakai]]
- maritime trade networks controlled by Seluqar
- distribution to major cities across Thalassar

From Seluqar, it reaches:

- temple markets
- luxury perfume industries
- foreign trade partners

It is often traded in graded forms, with higher purity commanding significantly higher prices.

---

# Cultural Significance

Masari Resin is deeply tied to ritual and identity.

It is used in:

- temple incense offerings
- port-city purification rites
- ceremonial burnings

Notably:

- Duskbloom sawdust is burned with Masari Resin during keel-layings
- the smoke is believed to bind crafted vessels to the sea

Among Masari peoples, the scent of Resin is associated with:

- ancestry
- continuity
- the living presence of the jungle

# Dangers and Limitations

- overharvesting can damage or kill source trees
- dependence on long growth cycles
- vulnerability to exploitation by external powers
- fluctuating yields based on environmental conditions

Economic pressure from Seluqar demand increases risk of unsustainable practices.
