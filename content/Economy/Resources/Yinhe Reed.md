# Yinhe Reed

**Resource Type:** Agricultural / Cultural / Artisan Material  
**Primary Region(s):** [[Kiyora]] — riverbanks, wetlands, and irrigated lowlands within the **Qinghe** and **Fenghe** river basins near [[Qinghe]] and [[Heiwah]]  
**Major Production Centers:** Reed marshes near [[Qinghe]] (Qinghe basin), cultivated beds in [[Heiwah]], instrument workshops in [[Fenghai]] (Fenghe basin)  
**Controlling Groups:** Reed cultivators, instrument-making guilds, performing arts circles, local agricultural stewards  
**Primary Uses:** Musical instruments, acoustic components, ceremonial chimes, decorative soundcraft  
**Trade Value:** Common → Valuable (refined forms)

---

# Overview

Yinhe Reed is a specialized river-grown plant known for its unique acoustic properties, making it the foundational material for many of Kiyora’s musical instruments. Cultivated in slow-moving waterways and controlled wetlands, it produces long, hollow stalks with a natural resonance that allows for clear, sustained tones.

Unlike [[Hezhu Bamboo]], which is valued for strength and structural use, Yinhe Reed is prized for sensitivity and tonal quality. When properly harvested and cured, its fibers produce a warm, stable sound that is difficult to replicate with other materials. This makes it essential for flutes, reed instruments, and tonal components in stringed and percussive designs.

Beyond its practical use, Yinhe Reed holds cultural importance. It is closely associated with music, reflection, and controlled expression—qualities that align with broader Kiyoran values. From rural performances to refined court compositions, its sound is woven into the auditory identity of the region.

# Geographic Distribution

Yinhe Reed grows in:

• riverbanks along the **Qinghe River**  
• marshlands near [[Qinghe]]  
• floodplain wetlands around [[Heiwah]]  
• secondary wetland zones connected to the **Fenghe River**  
• controlled aquatic beds in irrigated regions

It thrives in:

• shallow, slow-moving freshwater  
• nutrient-rich sediment  
• stable water levels with minimal disruption

These conditions allow:

• even growth of hollow stalks  
• consistent fiber density  
• optimal acoustic quality

Unlike wild reeds, Yinhe is often:

• cultivated in managed beds  
• selectively harvested for quality

# Production

Yinhe Reed cultivation requires environmental control and careful timing.

---

### Cultivation

• planted in shallow wetland beds or natural marsh zones  
• water levels are regulated using river-fed irrigation systems  
• density is managed to prevent overcrowding

---

### Harvesting

• stalks are cut at specific maturity stages  
• timing affects tonal quality and structural integrity  
• only straight, unwarped reeds are selected for high-grade use

---

### Initial Treatment

• reeds are cleaned and dried  
• outer layers may be trimmed or preserved depending on use  
• material is sorted by size, thickness, and quality

Production is:

• moderately scalable  
• highly dependent on environmental stability  
• quality-driven rather than volume-driven

# Processing and Transport

---

### Processing

Yinhe Reed is refined into:

• hollow tubes for flutes and wind instruments  
• split reeds for tonal components  
• shaped pieces for chimes and resonance structures

Advanced processing includes:

• curing to stabilize tone  
• polishing inner surfaces  
• tuning through precise cutting and shaping

---

### Transport

Yinhe Reed is transported:

• from wetlands along the **Qinghe basin** to workshops in [[Fenghai]]  
• via river routes connected to the **Qinghe** and **Fenghe** systems  
• in bundled, protected forms to prevent warping

High-quality reeds are:

• handled carefully  
• reserved for skilled artisans
