# Overview

Indrava Blue is a deep, blue dye derived from a cultivated plant grown in the warm, irrigated lowlands of the region. Known for its unmatched saturation and permanence, it is the defining pigment of the subcontinental textile economy and one of its most recognizable exports.

Unlike lesser dyes that fade or wash out over time, Indrava binds permanently to fiber when properly processed, producing a color that deepens with age rather than diminishing. This stability has made it indispensable in textile production, particularly in combination with **Velari Weave**, where it reveals intricate patterns and layered designs with exceptional clarity.

Its value lies not only in its color, but in the complexity of its production. Indrava must be processed through controlled fermentation and oxidation cycles to develop its characteristic blue, requiring both expertise and time. As a result, high-quality Indrava is as much a product of knowledge as it is of agriculture.

Across the world, Indrava Blue has become synonymous with craftsmanship, refinement, and origin. A garment dyed in true Indrava is not just colored—it is identified.

• controlled agricultural zones within the [[Varanji Basin]]  
• foothill terraces along the [[Chandral Range]]  
• select regions near [[Fenghara]] where soil conditions are optimal

The plant requires:

• warm temperatures  
• consistent irrigation  
• nutrient-rich soil

It thrives in regions with:

• stable water access  
• long growing seasons  
• established agricultural infrastructure

Because of these requirements, Indrava cultivation is limited to specific river-fed zones.

# Production

Indrava Blue production is a multi-stage process combining agriculture and chemistry.

---

### Cultivation

• plants are grown in dense, irrigated plots  
• leaves are harvested at peak maturity  
• timing directly affects pigment quality

---

### Fermentation

• harvested leaves are soaked in controlled vats  
• fermentation releases pigment precursors  
• conditions must be carefully managed to avoid spoilage

---

### Oxidation

• extracted liquid is exposed to air  
• chemical transformation produces the deep blue color  
• repeated cycles deepen pigment strength

---

### Drying and Formation

• pigment is reduced into paste or solid form  
• graded by intensity and purity  
• prepared for transport or immediate use

Production is:

• labor-intensive  
• knowledge-dependent  
• highly sensitive to environmental conditions

# Economic Importance

Indrava Blue is one of the most important trade pigments in the known world.

It supports:

• the entire Velari textile industry  
• artisan dye houses and pattern guilds  
• high-value export markets

Cities such as [[Kavartha]] and [[Varanji]] depend heavily on Indrava production for economic strength.

Its importance is amplified by:

> its irreplaceability—true Indrava cannot be easily replicated
