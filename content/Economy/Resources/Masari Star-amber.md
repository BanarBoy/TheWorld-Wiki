---

# Overview

Masari Star-Ambers are translucent amber gemstones formed from ancient resin deposits within the dense jungles of the [[Masari Islands]]. Their defining feature is the presence of fine, reflective inclusions that catch and scatter light, creating a faint internal shimmer reminiscent of a star-filled sky.

Unlike common amber, Star-Ambers possess unusual clarity and structural stability, allowing them to be cut, polished, and shaped without losing their internal brilliance. Each piece is unique, with its pattern of inclusions often treated as a signature of origin or meaning.

They are prized not only for their beauty, but for their rumored connection to the deep jungle itself.

> No two Star-Ambers hold the same sky.

---

# Geographic Distribution

Star-Ambers are found primarily in:

• shallow riverbeds within Masari jungle interiors  
• slow-moving jungle waterways rich in ancient sediment  
• exposed deposits along eroded banks after seasonal rains

Key zones include:

• interior river systems far from coastal settlements  
• dense canopy regions where ancient resin deposits accumulated  
• flood-cleared channels that reveal buried amber pockets

Their formation is tied to:

• prehistoric resin from Masari hardwoods  
• long-term burial and mineral infusion  
• pressure and environmental stabilization over centuries

---

# Production

Star-Ambers are not mined in the traditional sense—they are **gathered and recovered**.

Harvesting methods include:

• river-diving in shallow jungle waterways  
• sifting sediment after seasonal floods  
• careful extraction from exposed banks

Gatherers must:

• identify true Star-Amber by internal shimmer  
• avoid brittle or incomplete pieces  
• work in hazardous jungle conditions

Production is:

• low-volume  
• labor-intensive  
• dependent on seasonal river cycles

---

# Processing and Transport

Raw Star-Amber is processed by skilled Masari artisans.

---

### Processing

• cleaned and stabilized after extraction  
• cut into beads, seals, or shaped forms  
• polished to enhance internal reflections

Higher-grade pieces are:

• minimally altered to preserve natural structure  
• often left partially raw to showcase inclusions

---

### Transport

Star-Ambers move through:

• jungle river routes to coastal settlements  
• Seluqar-linked maritime trade networks

Due to their value, they are:

• transported in small, high-security shipments  
• often traded through trusted intermediaries

---

# Economic Importance

Star-Ambers are a **high-value, low-volume luxury export**.

They:

• command strong prices in foreign markets  
• contribute to Masari artisan economies  
• reinforce the islands’ reputation for rare materials

Their value comes from:

> rarity, beauty, and perceived mystique

---

# Controlling Powers

Control is localized and knowledge-based.

Primary actors include:

• Masari gatherer clans controlling river zones  
• river-diver guilds specializing in recovery  
• artisan houses shaping and finishing pieces

Seluqar traders handle broader distribution but do not control production.

---

# Trade Networks

Star-Ambers are exported to:

• Valecaryn noble markets  
• Seluqar trade hubs  
• foreign courts and collectors

They are especially valued in:

• jewelry and adornment  
• signet seals and decorative objects  
• arcane and ritual contexts

---

# Cultural Significance

Among the Masari, Star-Ambers are believed to:

• hold echoes of ancient jungle life  
• carry memory of the land  
• reflect unseen or spiritual forces

They are often used in:

• protective charms  
• ceremonial objects  
• personal tokens of status or connection

---

# Arcane Properties (Rumored)

Some traditions claim Star-Ambers can:

• stabilize minor magical workings  
• act as weak arcane foci  
• retain impressions of past energy

While not powerful artifacts, they are valued by:

• minor practitioners  
• scholars  
• collectors of unusual materials

# Dangers and Limitations

• extremely limited supply  
• difficult and hazardous extraction  
• vulnerability to environmental disruption  
• high risk of counterfeit materials

Additionally:

• overharvesting can damage river ecosystems  
• lower-quality amber is often passed as Star-Amber
